Who are these people,

02 / Acculturation strategies

How do migrants navigate
cultural adaptation?

How do you navigate culture when you move to a new country? Berry identified four strategies. Blend in completely — that's Assimilation. Hold on to your roots and reject the new — Separation. Let go of both — Marginalization. Or do both at once, maintaining who you are while adopting what's useful — Integration.

04 / The central finding

Do acculturation strategies
predict engagement?

Berry's model is clear: Integration is the gold standard. Keep your culture, adopt the new one. Best of both worlds — best outcomes.

But empirical studies tell a different story. In Australia, in the UK — Assimilation predicted engagement better than Integration. Not theory. Data.

We asked: does that hold in forced migration? In Latin America? We ran Pearson correlations for each strategy against engagement.

Correlations: acculturation strategies × total work engagement
Strategy Correlation (r) p-value Interpretation
Assimilation +0.177* .019 Small but significant
Integration +0.026 ns (.737) Practically zero
Separation −0.032 ns (.676) No effect
Marginalization +0.046 ns (.548) No effect

Integration — the strategy 76% chose — has zero relationship with engagement. The only strategy that predicts it is Assimilation. Small effect, but real. Consistent with every prior study in labor migration.

Finding 01 · Pearson correlations, N = 176 · Consistent with Lu et al. (2011) and Turska & Mochnacka (2016)

Assimilation matters — but barely (r = .17). Something else is doing heavier lifting. We kept looking.

t(174) = 4.43, p < .001. Effect size d ≈ 0.68 — moderate, by conventional standards.

The same group also reported less acculturation stress (M = 1.58 vs 1.99, p = .008). Maintaining professional continuity doesn't just protect engagement — it also reduces the friction of cultural adaptation.

06 / Moderation analysis

Does working in your field buffer
the impact of stress?

We knew field continuity predicted engagement. We knew stress reduced it. But did one change the other?

A moderation model tests exactly that: does the effect of stress on engagement depend on whether you kept your field? We built three nested models to find out.

07 / Conclusion

The story
the data tell

In forced migration, how you navigate culture matters less than whether you could stay yourself professionally.

Professional
identity
Occupational continuity is the most robust predictor of engagement — more than acculturation strategies, more than accumulated stress, more than time spent in the host country.
Two independent
pathways
Occupational mismatch elevates stress and reduces engagement, but through separate channels. Stress has its own direct effect (r = −.17), not mediated by field continuity.
Berry
revisited
Integration — preferred by 76% — does not predict higher engagement. Marginalization — feared by theory — does not predict higher stress either. Forced migration context substantially qualifies the classical acculturation model.
Limitations: non-probabilistic sample (N = 176), cross-sectional design, data collected 2017–2018. Results are exploratory and should be interpreted cautiously before generalization. Future research should examine migration legal status and occupational adjustment as potential moderators.

That's the finding. Not elegant theory — just what the data said.

This piece is an extended analysis of original data collected for a Master's thesis in Organizational Psychology (Universidad de Belgrano, Argentina, 2021). The acculturation strategies × engagement analysis and the moderation model are new and were not part of the original thesis. Analysis in Python (pandas, scipy, statsmodels).

Instruments: Acculturation Stress Scale for Latin American Immigrants (Ruiz et al., 2011) — 24 items, 6 factors, α = .92; Utrecht Work Engagement Scale–Short Version (UWES-9; Schaufeli, Bakker & Salanova, 2006) — vigor (α = .82), dedication (α = .86), absorption (α = .80). Psychometric properties of the UWES-9 validated in Latin American samples: Mexico (Hernández et al., 2016), Ecuador (Portalanza et al., 2017), Puerto Rico (Rodríguez et al., 2014).
